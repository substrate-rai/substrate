# Storytelling Craft — Reference for Narrative Agents

Ingested 2026-03-09. Sources: Alan Moore *Writing for Comics* (2003), Syd Field *Screenplay* (2005).

## Alan Moore: Core Principles

### Idea vs. Plot
- The **idea** is what the story is *about* — not plot events but the essential theme
- Example: Swamp Thing #40 was *about* women's difficulties in masculine societies; the *plot* was possession in an old lodge
- Most stories lack a real idea beyond "conflict is interesting in itself"
- The **"So What?" test**: after describing any plot, ask "So what?" If the answer is just more plot, the story lacks resonance

### Structure
- Stories need defined shape for unity and completeness
- **Elliptical structure**: beginning mirrors ending (bookend unity)
- **In medias res**: start in the middle, move both ways in time
- **Multi-perspective**: each character tells from their vantage; full picture only when all combine (borrowed from García Márquez)
- **Transitions**: movement between scenes is the trickiest element — bad transitions break the reader's "hypnotic involvement." Techniques: overlapping dialogue, visual synchronicity, page-turn as scene change

### Storytelling as Hypnosis
- Fascinate with the first sentence, draw in with the second, achieve mild trance by the third
- Carry through narrative without waking them up
- Transitions are the weak points in the spell

### World Building
- Three fictional elements: characterization, environment, plot
- **Environment first**: plot and character motivations are largely determined by the world
- **Best method**: work out the world in exhaustive detail *before writing* — then let readers pick up details naturally (like absorbing a foreign culture while traveling)
- Moore's V for Vendetta process: created mass of world information, much never appearing in the strip, so he could talk about the world with "confidence and casual authority"
- Tiny authentic details (Louisiana bayou ecology for Swamp Thing) make worlds convincing

### Characterization
- Real people can't be summed up in 15 words — they change voice depending on audience, do things that seem out of character
- **Method acting approach**: physically embody characters — imagine the weight, temperature, speech impediments → produces distinctive dialogue
- Everyone in the story is a character, even background walkers

### Wordsmithing
- What separates interesting from dull isn't subject matter — it's arrangement of words
- **Verbal rhythm matters**: multi-syllable overload = jerky; long flowing imagery vs. short sparse sentences create different effects
- **Dialogue test**: read it aloud. If it sounds phony, it fails

### Advanced Principles
- **Avoid recognizable style**: when you detect your own tricks, you've overused them
- **Deliberate difficulty**: when praised for X, try the opposite
- **Work without safety net**: attempt things you're uncertain about
- **"Take risks. Fear nothing, especially failure."**

## Syd Field: Three-Act Paradigm

### Structure
- **Act I (Setup)** ~25%: character, dramatic premise, dramatic situation. First 10 pages are critical.
- **Act II (Confrontation)** ~50%: obstacle after obstacle. Longest and hardest.
- **Act III (Resolution)** ~25%: resolves the story. Resolution ≠ ending; resolution = solution.

### Plot Points
- **Plot Point I** (end of Act I): incident that hooks into action, spins it into Act II
- **Plot Point II** (end of Act II): incident that spins action into Act III
- These are structural anchors — know them before you write

### Character
- Character is **action**: what a person does is who they are, not what they say
- Two components: **interior** (biography from birth) and **exterior** (the story itself)
- Create character biography even if most never appears

### Working Method
- **Know your ending before you start writing** — the ending is the first thing to determine
- Then determine beginning, Plot Points I and II → four structural anchors
- Subject stated simply: "a person, in a place, doing a thing"
- **Dramatic need**: what does the character want? This drives everything

### The Scene
- Single most important element
- Every scene has **purpose** (advance story or reveal character, ideally both)
- Every scene has **context** (space) and **content** (what happens)
- Construction: identify purpose → place → time → components → write

## Application to Substrate

### For Objection! Cyber Court
- Moore's idea-vs-plot: each case should be *about* something (privacy vs. safety, trust vs. verification), not just *plot* (someone hacked something)
- Field's three-act: Investigation setup → Evidence gathering/confrontation → Resolution/verdict
- Plot points as case twists; know the truth before designing the investigation
- Hypnosis model for player engagement — never break the spell

### For All Narrative Work
- World-building depth: know far more than you show
- Method-acting characterization for agent voices
- Verbal rhythm consciousness in all writing
- Elliptical structure for blog posts and lore pieces
