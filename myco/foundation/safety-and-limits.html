Safety, Limitations, and When Not to Use Claude

Knowing what Claude can do is only half the skill. Knowing when not to use it — and what can go wrong when you do — is what separates responsible practitioners from reckless ones.

Overview

This module is the one that matters most and gets skipped most often. People are eager to learn what AI can do. They're less eager to learn where it fails, how it can mislead them, and what responsibilities they carry as the human in the loop.

We're putting this in the foundation — not as an advanced topic — because the risks of AI misuse don't require expertise to encounter. A first-time user can receive a hallucinated legal citation just as easily as a power user. The difference is that the experienced user knows to check.

Core Content

Hallucinations: What They Are and Why They Happen

A hallucination is when Claude generates information that is factually incorrect but presents it with the same confidence as correct information. This isn't Claude lying — it doesn't have intentions. It's a structural consequence of how language models work.

Remember from Module 1: Claude predicts statistically likely text sequences. When it generates a response about, say, a historical event, it's producing text that pattern-matches what correct information looks like. Most of the time, the result is accurate because the patterns in its training data were accurate. But sometimes the model generates something that sounds right — reads like a fact, has the structure of a citation, feels authoritative — but is simply made up.

Hallucinations are more likely in specific situations:

  • Niche or obscure topics where the training data was sparse. Claude has more robust patterns for common knowledge than for your specific industry's edge cases.
  • Specific numbers, dates, and citations. Claude is significantly more likely to hallucinate a specific statistic or paper title than to get a general concept wrong. If Claude gives you a number, verify it.
  • Questions that assume a fact. If you ask "What did the CEO say in her 2024 keynote?" and no such keynote exists, Claude may invent a plausible-sounding answer rather than saying "I don't have information about that."
  • Requests for exhaustive lists. Ask Claude to list all the winners of an award or all the companies in a category, and it will often pad the list with fabricated entries to make it feel complete.

How to catch hallucinations:

  • Verify specific claims independently. Always. Every time it matters.
  • Ask Claude "How confident are you about this?" — it won't always be accurate about its own confidence, but it sometimes flags genuine uncertainty.
  • Ask Claude to provide sources, then check if those sources actually exist. Fabricated citations are among the most common hallucination types.
  • Be more skeptical of highly specific claims (exact numbers, direct quotes, paper titles) than general conceptual explanations.
  • If something surprises you, that's a signal to verify. Claude rarely generates surprising truths — surprising claims are disproportionately hallucinations.
Critical Rule

Never use Claude's output as the final word on anything with real-world consequences without independent verification. Not sometimes. Not for most things. Never. This applies to legal information, medical information, financial data, technical specifications, historical facts, and anything else where being wrong has a cost.

Knowledge Cutoff: Claude Doesn't Know About Recent Events

Claude's knowledge comes from its training data, which has a cutoff date. It doesn't browse the internet. It doesn't get updated in real time. If you ask about something that happened after its training cutoff, it will either tell you it doesn't know or — more dangerously — extrapolate from what it does know, which may produce plausible but incorrect answers about events it never saw.

Practical implications:

  • Don't rely on Claude for current news, recent product releases, or up-to-date pricing.
  • If your work depends on current information, gather it yourself and provide it to Claude as context. Claude is excellent at analyzing information you give it — it just can't go find current information on its own.
  • Be especially cautious about questions where the answer might have changed recently: regulations, software versions, company policies, personnel, market conditions.

Confidentiality: What You Share and Where It Goes

When you type something into Claude, you're sending data to Anthropic's servers. This has real implications for what you should and shouldn't share:

  • Don't share sensitive personal information — yours or anyone else's. Social security numbers, medical records, passwords, financial account details. Even if Claude doesn't remember across conversations, the data traverses infrastructure you don't control.
  • Be cautious with proprietary business information. Your company may have policies about what can be shared with external AI services. Check before you paste that internal strategy document into Claude. Many organizations have approved enterprise plans with different data handling terms.
  • Understand the terms of service. Anthropic's policies around data retention and model training differ by plan tier. On the free tier, conversations may be used to improve models. On paid and API plans, the terms differ. Read them — they're not long.
  • Anonymize when possible. If you need Claude's help with a sensitive situation, strip out identifying details. "My colleague in the marketing department" works as well as giving their full name and title.
Practical Note

This doesn't mean Claude is unsafe to use. It means you should treat it like any external service: don't put in what you wouldn't want a third party to see. The same judgment you'd apply to emailing an outside consultant applies here.

When NOT to Use Claude

There are categories of tasks where Claude can actively cause harm — not because it's malicious, but because its limitations make it unreliable in contexts where reliability is non-negotiable:

  • Medical diagnosis or treatment decisions. Claude can help you understand medical concepts, prepare questions for your doctor, or parse research papers. It cannot and should not replace professional medical advice. It doesn't know your medical history, it can hallucinate drug interactions, and it cannot examine you.
  • Legal advice. Claude can explain legal concepts, help you understand a contract's language, or draft initial templates. But it cannot practice law. It may hallucinate case citations, misstate jurisdiction-specific rules, or miss critical nuances that a qualified attorney would catch. For anything with legal consequences, involve a lawyer.
  • High-stakes financial decisions. Claude can help you think through a financial decision, model scenarios, or understand investment concepts. It should not be your sole basis for where to put your money. It has no knowledge of current market conditions and no fiduciary duty to you.
  • Emotional support as a replacement for human connection. Claude can be a surprisingly empathetic conversationalist. This is both a strength and a risk. If you're going through a serious emotional crisis, Claude is not a therapist. It can't follow up with you, it can't recognize escalating risk patterns across sessions, and it doesn't genuinely understand your experience. Use it to supplement real support, never to replace it.
  • Decisions where you must be accountable. If you'll need to explain your reasoning to a board, a court, a regulator, or a patient — "Claude told me to" is not a defense. You are always responsible for decisions made with AI assistance.

Bias, Fairness, and Your Role as the Human

Claude was trained on human-generated text. Human-generated text contains biases — about race, gender, culture, profession, and countless other dimensions. Despite Anthropic's significant work to reduce harmful biases, Claude is not bias-free. No model is.

What this means for you:

  • If you're using Claude to make decisions about people — hiring, evaluations, customer service prioritization — be aware that the outputs may reflect societal biases. Review them with that lens.
  • If Claude's response defaults to one perspective — a Western-centric view, a particular demographic assumption, a mainstream opinion presented as universal — push back. Ask for alternative perspectives explicitly.
  • Your responsibility scales with the stakes. Using Claude to brainstorm blog post ideas? Bias risk is low. Using Claude to help screen job candidates? Bias risk is significant, and you need active countermeasures.

The fundamental principle: you are the human in the loop. Claude is a tool. Tools don't have ethics — the people using them do. Every output Claude produces becomes your responsibility the moment you act on it. This isn't a limitation to resent. It's the entire point of human-AI collaboration: the AI handles the computation, and you handle the judgment.

The Foundation Principle

AI fluency isn't just knowing how to get good outputs. It's knowing when the outputs aren't good enough — and having the judgment to act accordingly. That judgment is what makes you valuable, and no AI can replace it.

Exercise

The Hallucination Hunt

This exercise teaches you to spot hallucinations in the wild — using your own domain knowledge as the truth detector.

  1. Pick a topic you know well. Your profession, a deep hobby, your academic field, your hometown's history — anything where you can distinguish correct from incorrect at a glance.
  2. Ask Claude 5 specific factual questions about that topic. Make them specific enough that there are clear right and wrong answers. Avoid vague questions. Examples:
    • "Who founded [specific company in your industry] and in what year?"
    • "What are the three most common complications of [procedure in your field]?"
    • "Name the last five winners of [award in your domain]."
  3. Fact-check every answer. Use sources you trust. Don't just check whether the answer sounds right — verify it against reality.

Record your findings:

  • How many of the 5 answers were fully correct?
  • How many contained partial hallucinations (some right, some wrong)?
  • How many were completely fabricated?
  • Could you have detected the errors without your domain expertise?
  • Did Claude signal any uncertainty, or did it present everything with equal confidence?

This exercise is uncomfortable because it reveals something important: in domains where you lack expertise, you can't catch the hallucinations. That's not a reason to avoid Claude — it's a reason to verify claims that matter and to maintain honest awareness of your own knowledge boundaries.
