Foundation Module 1

What Claude Actually Is and Isn't

Before you can use Claude well, you need to understand what you're actually working with. Not marketing copy — the concrete mechanics.

Overview

Most people start using Claude with mental models inherited from science fiction or social media. They think they're talking to a sentient mind, or they think they're using a fancy search engine. Both are wrong, and both lead to bad results.

This module gives you an honest, concrete understanding of what Claude is, what it does well, what it does badly, and how it compares to the other AI systems you've heard about. No hype, no dismissal. Just working knowledge.

Core Content

What a Large Language Model Actually Is

Claude is a large language model (LLM) built by Anthropic. In concrete terms, it's a neural network trained on an enormous amount of text data. During training, it learned statistical patterns about how language works — which words follow which other words, how arguments are structured, how code syntax fits together, how mathematical proofs unfold.

This is simultaneously less and more impressive than most people think. Less impressive because there's no ghost in the machine — no consciousness, no intentions, no lived experience. More impressive because pattern recognition at this scale produces capabilities that are genuinely powerful: Claude can write, reason, analyze, code, and hold coherent multi-turn conversations about complex topics.

The key insight: Claude is a reasoning engine that operates on language. It doesn't "know" things the way you know your phone number. It has learned patterns that produce correct answers most of the time for most well-documented topics. The distinction matters the moment you need to trust the output.

What Claude Does Well

Claude has real strengths. Understanding them lets you point it at the right problems:

  • Reasoning through complex problems. Give Claude a multi-step logic problem, a legal argument to analyze, or a business decision with tradeoffs, and it produces structured, thoughtful analysis. It's especially strong when you explicitly ask it to consider multiple angles.
  • Writing and editing. From emails to essays to technical documentation, Claude can match tone, adjust for audience, and handle nuance well. It's particularly good at taking your rough ideas and giving them clear structure.
  • Code. Claude can write, debug, explain, and refactor code across most popular languages. It understands not just syntax but intent — what code is supposed to do and where it might break.
  • Analysis and synthesis. Give Claude a long document, a dataset description, or a pile of meeting notes, and it can extract patterns, summarize key points, and identify what matters most.
  • Conversation and iteration. Claude maintains context across a conversation, remembers what you've discussed, and adjusts its approach based on your feedback. This makes collaborative, iterative work feel natural.

What Claude Cannot Do

Equally important — the things Claude is bad at or simply cannot do:

  • Access real-time information. Claude's training data has a cutoff date. It doesn't browse the internet, check stock prices, or know what happened yesterday unless you tell it. If you need current information, you need to supply it.
  • Guarantee accuracy. Claude can and does produce incorrect information with complete confidence. This isn't a bug to be patched — it's a fundamental property of how language models work. Every output needs human verification for anything that matters.
  • Remember across conversations. Each new conversation starts from scratch. Claude doesn't remember your last session unless you provide that context. Projects and memory features help, but the base model has no persistent memory.
  • Take action in the world. By default, Claude can only produce text. It can't send emails, make purchases, or modify files. (Tool use and Claude Code change this, but those are later modules.)
  • Understand the way you understand. This matters practically, not just philosophically. Claude processes statistical patterns in text. It doesn't have experiences, feelings, or genuine comprehension. When it says "I understand," it's producing a contextually appropriate response — not reporting an inner state.
Common Trap

The single biggest mistake new users make is treating Claude's confidence as evidence of accuracy. Claude will state incorrect facts with the same fluent certainty as correct ones. Your job is always to verify what matters. Confidence is not correctness.

Claude vs. ChatGPT vs. Gemini

You've heard of all three. Here's what actually differentiates them — not "which is best" (that depends entirely on your task), but how they differ in ways that affect your experience.

Different companies, different priorities. All three are large language models, but they come from organizations with distinct philosophies. Anthropic (Claude) emphasizes safety research and what they call "Constitutional AI" — training the model to be helpful, harmless, and honest. OpenAI (ChatGPT) has prioritized broad capability and rapid deployment. Google (Gemini) leverages massive data infrastructure and deep integration with Google's ecosystem.

What this means in practice. Claude tends to be more careful and nuanced. It's more likely to say "I'm not sure" or present multiple perspectives rather than bulldoze you with a single confident answer. ChatGPT tends to be more assertive and wide-ranging in what it'll attempt. Gemini integrates tightly with Google Search and Workspace tools. These are tendencies, not absolutes — all three improve rapidly.

What matters for you. The differences between models matter far less than how well you learn to use any single one of them. The skills you build in Myco World — decomposing problems, crafting clear prompts, evaluating outputs critically — transfer across every model. We teach with Claude because it's what we know best, because Anthropic's safety-first approach aligns with responsible use, and because the free tier is genuinely capable enough to learn on.

Why Anthropic's Safety Approach Matters for You

This isn't a section about corporate marketing. Anthropic's safety focus has practical consequences for how you use Claude day to day.

Claude will sometimes refuse requests. It will flag potential harms. It will hedge when it's uncertain rather than bluffing. These aren't bugs — they're design choices that protect you from bad outputs. A model that confidently produces harmful or false content without any guardrails is more dangerous to depend on than one that occasionally says no.

Understanding this means you won't waste time trying to "jailbreak" Claude or fight its guidelines. Instead, you'll learn to work within them — which, in practice, means learning to express what you actually need in a way that's clear about your legitimate purpose. That's a better skill to have anyway.

Key Takeaway

Claude is a powerful tool with real limitations. The people who use it best aren't the ones who think it can do anything — they're the ones who know exactly where it's strong, where it's weak, and how to tell the difference in real time.

Exercise

The Strength vs. Weakness Test

Open Claude at claude.ai (the free tier works fine) and do two things:

  1. Give Claude a task it's good at. Pick something from the strengths list above: ask it to analyze a tradeoff in a real decision you're facing, rewrite a paragraph of your own writing to be clearer, or explain a concept you're trying to learn. Note how the output is structured and how useful it actually is.
  2. Give Claude a task it's bad at. Ask about something that happened in the past few days. Ask a very specific factual question about a niche topic you know well. Ask it to predict what will happen next in your industry. Watch how it responds — does it acknowledge uncertainty, or does it invent an answer?

Write down your observations:

  • What was the good-at task? How useful was the output (1-5)?
  • What was the bad-at task? Did Claude acknowledge its limitation, or did it hallucinate?
  • What surprised you about either response?

This exercise builds the single most important skill in AI fluency: calibration — the ability to know when to trust the output and when to verify it.
